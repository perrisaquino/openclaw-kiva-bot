# I Built a Recording App for Dancers. Here's What It Actually Does.

*The first post in a series about building Cyphr — a professional practice ecosystem for movement artists.*

---

Last week, I was standing in Death Valley at sunset.

The wildflowers were blooming — something that happens maybe once a decade out there. The Golden Mountains were lit up behind me. People were scattered around, quiet, taking it all in.

And I noticed something. The way the light was hitting a specific spot near the mountains. The way the space felt like it was holding its breath.

I put my headphones in. Opened Cyphr. Hit record.

Three seconds. That's all it took to go from noticing something beautiful to being inside a creative moment with it. No speakers blasting. No disturbing the sixty-year-old couple twenty feet away. No fumbling with audio sync or camera settings.

Just headphones, music, and movement in a place that demanded it.

My girlfriend Glenny sat on a stone wall above and filmed from a distance. The mountains behind me. The sunset going. People walking by who had no idea I was hearing anything at all.

That footage — clean audio, perfectly synced, the truth of what I heard and how I moved — exists now because the tool got out of the way fast enough for the moment to happen.

This is what I built Cyphr to do. And it's not what most people assume when they hear "recording app for dancers."

---

## What People Think Cyphr Is

When I tell someone I built a recording app for dancers, they usually think one of two things:

1. "Oh, so it's like TikTok but for dance?"
2. "So it helps you make better dance videos for Instagram?"

No. And no.

Cyphr isn't a content creation tool. Content is a byproduct — the same way a journal entry might become an essay someday, but you don't write journals to produce essays. You write journals to think.

Cyphr is a practice tool.

Specifically, it's the practice tool that didn't exist for movement artists. The thing that DaVinci Resolve is for filmmakers, that Logic Pro is for musicians, that a sketchbook is for visual artists — Cyphr is that, for people who move.

---

## What Cyphr Actually Does

Here's the simple version: you put in your headphones, pick a song, hit record, and dance. The music plays directly in your ears. The recording captures it perfectly synced. When you watch it back, what you hear is exactly what you heard while you were moving.

That's it. That's the foundation.

But here's why that matters more than it sounds:

**Your practice finally has a mirror that doesn't lie.**

Every dancer knows the gap. The gap between what you *felt* during a session and what you *see* when you watch it back. Usually, that gap is made worse by bad audio, weird angles, a phone that fell over, or a Bluetooth speaker that was too quiet to actually sync with your movement.

With Cyphr, the gap closes. Not because the tool makes you look better — it doesn't. It makes you look *true*. The audio matches. The recording runs as long as you need it to. The footage shows exactly what happened.

And when you can see the truth of where you're at, you can actually do something about it.

## The Training Tool That Didn't Exist

I've been dancing for over a decade. In that time, I've tried every recording setup imaginable:

- Phone propped against a wall with a Bluetooth speaker (audio echoes, phone falls, neighbors complain)
- Two devices — one recording, one playing music — synced manually in editing (this takes hours and the sync always drifts)
- Dancing in complete silence and adding music later (your body can tell. The musicality disappears when you're not actually hearing the track)
- Renting studio space at $40/hour (you're too stressed about the clock to actually practice)

Every one of these setups does the same thing: it puts a wall between you and your practice. By the time you've dealt with the technology, the creative impulse is gone. The thing you wanted to capture — that authentic, unconscious expression — has been replaced by the performance of "trying to record."

This is the problem Cyphr was built to solve. Not "how do I make better content?" but "how do I remove every barrier between me and the truth of my movement?"

## The Skill of Noticing

Here's something that changed how I think about this tool.

The best artists I know — dancers, painters, musicians, photographers — share a quality that has nothing to do with technical skill. It's the ability to *notice* things. To see a moment, a space, a quality of light, a feeling in the air, and recognize it as meaningful.

But noticing something isn't enough. You have to be able to *act* on what you notice before it disappears.

That's what happened in Death Valley. I noticed the light. I noticed the space. And because Cyphr lets me go from zero to recording in three seconds — headphones in, app open, go — I could act on that impulse before it faded.

Most of the time, those impulses die. You see a beautiful wall on your walk home. You feel a song hit different on the train. You pass a space that's begging for movement. But the friction of setting up a recording — speaker, tripod, angle, audio — kills it. By the time you're ready, the moment is gone.

Cyphr turns the skill of noticing into captured moments. It makes your entire environment a potential practice space. The park. The parking garage at midnight. Your bedroom at 2 AM when you can't sleep but you can feel the music.

The world becomes your studio.

## Making the Unconscious Conscious

There's a deeper layer to this that I didn't expect when I started building.

When you can reliably capture your practice — when the footage is honest, the audio is real, and you're not performing for the camera because you forgot it was there — something interesting starts to happen.

You start seeing yourself.

Not the version of you that shows up when you know you're being filmed. The real one. The unconscious patterns. The way your right arm always does that thing on the bass drop. The way your groove shifts when the melody changes. The tendencies you didn't know you had.

And when you can *see* those patterns, you can start making conscious choices about them. Keep this. Develop that. Break this habit. Lean into that impulse.

That's how you find your style. Not by copying someone else's movement, but by watching your own honest footage enough times that your signature starts to emerge from the noise.

Cyphr doesn't make you a better dancer. Your practice makes you a better dancer. Cyphr just makes sure you can actually see what's happening in your practice — clearly, honestly, over time.

## Building an Archive You Can Navigate

One of the people who shaped my thinking about this tool is a dancer named Shaun who's been in the scene for decades. His biggest regret? Not documenting his journey.

He doesn't wish he'd made more content. He wishes he had an archive. A body of work he could look back on and say: "That's where I was five years ago. That's when this started to shift. That's the session where something unlocked."

Cyphr is designed to be that archive. Not a camera roll of random clips with bad audio that you'll never look at again. A structured practice journal where every session is documented honestly and you can navigate your own development over time.

Where have you been? Where are you now? Where do you want to go?

Those questions require evidence. And evidence requires a tool that captures truth consistently.

---

## Why I Built This

I'm a dancer who couldn't find a professional tool for my practice.

I tried everything. Every workaround, every app, every setup. And every single one of them treated dance like a hobby. Like something casual. Something that didn't deserve real infrastructure.

But movement is a discipline. Freestyle is a discipline. Choreography is a discipline. They deserve a tool that takes them as seriously as Logic takes music or DaVinci takes film.

So I built one.

Cyphr isn't perfect yet. I'm still developing features, still learning from the community, still testing it in places like Death Valley to see what breaks and what holds up. But the core is there: put in your headphones, hit record, and focus on what matters — the movement.

Everything else disappears.
