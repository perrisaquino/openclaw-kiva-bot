# Why I'm Building an App for Dancers (And What It's Teaching Me About Everything)

*The story of CyphrCam - and what happens when you stop talking about solutions and start building them*

---

## The Catalyst

I've had the same stupid problem for twenty years.

You put on headphones, hit record on your phone, and start dancing. Later, when you watch the footage back, the audio is completely off. Your movements don't match the beat you were feeling. The music you recorded through your phone's microphone sounds like garbage compared to what was pumping through your headphones.

Every dancer knows this frustration. We've all got hundreds of videos sitting in our camera rolls that we'll never post because the thought of syncing audio for hours makes us want to throw our phones across the room.

I've been dancing since I was nine years old. I'm 29 now. That's twenty years of this problem existing, twenty years of thinking someone smarter than me would solve it, twenty years of accepting workarounds for something that should be simple: recording yourself dance to music.

But here's what finally broke my patience: I spent the last five years building a marketing agency, talking about tools and systems and growth strategies. I got good at helping other people solve their problems. Meanwhile, I was living with my own daily frustration and doing nothing about it.

I realized I had become someone who talks about solutions instead of building them.

That's when I decided to stop waiting and start learning Swift.

---

## The Investigation

The headphone sync problem isn't just a technical issue. It's a perfect metaphor for everything I've learned about building for your actual community versus building for an imagined market.

For five years, I was surrounded by entrepreneurs and marketers who could talk endlessly about customer pain points and product-market fit. But most of them were building for people they'd never met, solving problems they'd never lived with.

I was doing the same thing. I had marketing skills, I understood systems and growth, but I was applying them to other people's visions. I felt split between two identities: the business mind that could analyze markets and the artistic mind that understood what dancers actually need.

This split definition was killing me. In marketing circles, my artistry was invisible. My twenty years of dance experience, my time with Jabbawockeez, my deep understanding of street dance culture - none of that mattered when we were talking about conversion funnels and customer acquisition costs.

I started hiding that part of myself. It felt like I had to choose: be taken seriously as a business person or be authentic as an artist. The marketing world I was in didn't have space for both.

But dancers live with real problems every day. We need tools that actually work, not features that look good in demos. We need solutions built by people who understand the craft, not engineers guessing at what we might want.

The headphone sync issue is universal. I've had countless conversations with dancers who have hundreds of videos they'll never share because editing audio is such a pain. It affects practice sessions, content creation, how we share our art with each other. It's a daily barrier between what we feel in our bodies and what we can show the world.

Here's what I started noticing: the tools built for dancers are usually created by people who don't dance. They solve problems engineers think dancers have, not problems dancers actually live with. Apps focus on filters, effects, social features - everything except the basic functionality we actually need.

It's the same pattern everywhere. Tools built for communities by outsiders miss the mark because they're optimized for features that sound good in meetings, not workflows that serve the actual craft.

I realized that if I wanted tools that truly serve my community, I had to stop waiting for someone else to understand us and start building them myself.

---

## The Integration

Learning to code while maintaining my artistic identity has been the strangest experience of my life.

I'm doing everything myself: coding the app, designing the UI, writing the copy, building landing pages, conducting beta tests, creating content, managing community. I've accidentally deleted hundreds of lines of code and had to start from scratch. I've spent entire nights debugging audio synchronization algorithms.

But something interesting happened in the process. CyphrCam became more than a solution to a technical problem. It became my way of integrating everything I've learned - as a dancer, as a marketer, as someone who understands systems and community.

This project is teaching me what conscious creation looks like during chaotic times.

I made a video recently about blind joy versus conscious joy in the dance community. There's a difference between choosing joy as escape and choosing joy as resistance. Blind joy ignores what's happening around us. Conscious joy acknowledges the reality and creates something meaningful in response.

CyphrCam comes from that second place. It was born during 2025, when I felt completely disconnected from the dance community because of the political awareness I couldn't turn off. I watched spaces that were supposed to be sacred become complicit in systems that directly attack the communities that created this culture.

As a Filipino American dancer, I couldn't stay silent about what was happening. But speaking out made me feel isolated from the very community that had been my home for twenty years. Dance felt tainted. I was surrounded by people who chose silence over solidarity.

So I created CyphrCam as my way of saying thank you to dance itself, even when the community disappointed me. It's my version of resistance: building something that serves authentic creative expression when everything around us feels performative or complicit.

The tool represents everything I believe about how change actually happens. You don't wait for institutions to solve problems for you. You don't talk about solutions - you build them. You use whatever skills you've developed to create what your community actually needs.

This is what integration looks like for me: taking my marketing knowledge, my technical skills, my cultural awareness, my dance experience, and channeling all of it into something that serves authentic expression. Not hiding parts of myself to fit into someone else's idea of what an entrepreneur should be.

## The Invitation

CyphrCam launches soon, and I'm documenting this entire journey because I think the process matters as much as the product.

What I'm learning about building for your own community might be useful to other people who feel caught between different worlds - artists with business skills, entrepreneurs with cultural missions, creators who want to build tools instead of just content.

I'm sharing the technical discoveries, the business mistakes, the moments when imposter syndrome kicks in, and the breakthroughs when everything finally clicks. The intersection of artistry and technology needs more voices from the communities actually creating.

If you've ever felt stuck between creative expression and technical limitations, maybe we can solve some problems together. If you've ever wondered what conscious creation looks like during difficult times, maybe this journey resonates.

This is what I'm building: a tool that lets dancers put on headphones, hit record, and dance anywhere. Simple functionality that removes barriers between what we feel and what we can share.

But it's also what I'm becoming: someone who builds solutions instead of just talking about them. Someone who integrates all parts of themselves instead of hiding pieces to fit in. Someone who creates from consciousness rather than escaping into unconsciousness.

I'm still figuring this out as I go. But I'm done waiting for someone else to build what we need.

If this resonates, stick around. If you're a dancer, sign up for the waitlist. If you're building something that serves your community, let me know what you're working on.

The tools we need won't build themselves. But we can.
