# "What 20 Years of Dance Taught Me About Flow State"

**Target Length:** 10-15 minutes  
**Style:** Conversational, authority-building, vulnerable  
**Format:** Talking head + B-roll dance footage

[TALKING HEAD]

It's because everything around them is designed to pull them out of it.

[B-ROLL: Split screen showing TikTok dances vs. freestyle footage]

I'm Perris, and this is what two decades of dance taught me about flow state, creativity, and why the tools we use to create actually matter more than we think.

## PERSONAL STORY (0:30-3:00)

[TALKING HEAD]

So let me take you back to where this all started. I was nine years old, Filipino American kid in California, and I had this problem — I could not sit still.

[B-ROLL: Childhood photos, family footage if available, or recreation shots]

My mom put me in dance classes thinking it might help me focus. And honestly? I was terrible. Like, really bad. I was that kid who counted beats out loud, who thought about every single move, who was so in his head that he looked like a robot trying to learn how to be human.

[TALKING HEAD]

But there was this one moment — I remember it so clearly — I was maybe eleven, practicing in my garage, and this song came on. I don't even remember what song it was. But something shifted.

[B-ROLL: Recreation of garage practice, soft lighting, solo movement]

For maybe thirty seconds, I stopped thinking. I stopped counting. I stopped trying to remember choreography. My body just... knew what to do.

[TALKING HEAD]

And when it ended, I literally looked around like, "What just happened?" That was my first taste of flow state, and I've been chasing that feeling ever since.

[B-ROLL: Montage of practice footage over the years, progression of skill]

Twenty years later, I've learned that moment wasn't an accident. It wasn't magic. It was the result of specific conditions coming together in exactly the right way.

## WHAT FLOW STATE ACTUALLY FEELS LIKE (3:00-5:30)

[TALKING HEAD]

So what is flow state, really? Because everyone talks about it, but most explanations are either too academic or too vague to actually help.

[B-ROLL: Close-up footage of smooth, effortless movement]

Flow state is that moment where you stop thinking and just start being. You're so in tune with the flow, rhythm, consciousness, breath of it. You're not thinking about technicality or vocabulary — you're just feeling and your body knows what to do and you're trusting that process.

[TALKING HEAD]

It's like... imagine you're having a conversation with your best friend. You're not planning each word, you're not thinking about grammar, you're just responding naturally to what they're saying. That's flow state in movement.

[B-ROLL: Footage showing relaxed, conversational movement style]

But here's what's wild — in twenty years of dancing, I've probably experienced true flow state for maybe a total of an hour. Maybe.

[TALKING HEAD]

And that's not because I'm not good enough, or because I haven't practiced enough. It's because almost everything in our environment is designed to pull us out of flow state, not into it.

[B-ROLL: Quick cuts showing distractions — phone notifications, mirror adjustments, equipment setup]

Think about it. You're in your zone, you're feeling the music, your body's responding naturally, and then... you need to stop to adjust your camera. Or your music cuts out. Or someone walks in. Or you realize you weren't recording.

[TALKING HEAD]

Every single interruption breaks the spell. And the crazy part is, most of us have just accepted this as normal.

---

## THE PROBLEM WITH HOW TOOLS TREAT DANCERS (5:30-8:00)

[TALKING HEAD]

Here's where things get interesting. I've also been creating content for twenty years. I've used Adobe, DaVinci Resolve, Sony Vegas, Premiere, Descript — all the professional tools that filmmakers and content creators swear by.

[B-ROLL: Screenshots or footage of editing software interfaces]

And these are incredible tools. Logic Pro revolutionized music production. DaVinci Resolve is a filmmaker's dream. But dancers? Movement artists? We're still working with tools that weren't built for us.

[TALKING HEAD]

There's this massive difference between TikTok dance culture and freestyle culture that most people don't understand.

[B-ROLL: Split screen — TikTok dances vs. freestyle battle footage]

TikTok dance is about trends, virality, perfect execution of someone else's moves. It's surface level. And there's nothing wrong with that — it's fun, it's accessible, it brings people together.

[TALKING HEAD]

But freestyle? Freestyle is about finding your voice. It's about decades of craft. It's about authenticity. It's about answering the question: "Who are you when the music hits?"

[B-ROLL: Extended freestyle footage, showing personal style and expression]

And that question can't be answered with a fifteen-second clip optimized for likes.

[TALKING HEAD]

The problem is, all our tools are designed for the TikTok model. Quick clips, trends, surface-level sharing. There's nothing wrong with that, but where are the tools for the artist who wants to go deeper? 

[B-ROLL: Footage showing the frustration of setting up traditional recording equipment]

Music producers have Logic Pro — a complete creative ecosystem designed specifically for their craft. Filmmakers have DaVinci Resolve. But dancers? We're still trying to make tools work that were never built with our creative process in mind.

## WHAT 20 YEARS TAUGHT ME ABOUT CREATIVITY (8:00-10:30)

[TALKING HEAD]

So what did twenty years teach me about the creative process that you can't learn from YouTube tutorials or workshops?

[B-ROLL: Reflective footage, quiet practice moments]

First — process over perfection. Always. I spent years obsessing over clean execution, perfect technique, nailing every move exactly right. And you know what that got me? Technically proficient dance that had no soul.

[TALKING HEAD]

The most beautiful moments in movement aren't the perfect ones. They're the moments where you're so present with what you're feeling that technique becomes irrelevant.

[B-ROLL: Footage showing expressive, imperfect but emotional movement]

Second — the instant feedback loop is everything. Dance with headphones, see yourself immediately, iterate, evolve. The faster you can see what you just created and respond to it, the faster you grow as an artist.

[TALKING HEAD]

But here's the key — that feedback loop has to be frictionless. The moment you have to break your creative state to deal with technology, you've lost the thread.

[B-ROLL: Footage showing smooth, uninterrupted practice-to-playback flow]

Third — environment shapes consciousness more than we realize. This isn't just about having a good space to dance. It's about every single element either serving your flow state or pulling you away from it.

[TALKING HEAD]

I started asking myself this question about everything: "Does this serve flow state, or pull away from it?" And that question changed how I approached not just dance, but design, relationships, the tools I use, even the way I structure my days.

[B-ROLL: Clean, minimal practice space, intentional setup]

When you design your entire creative process around serving flow state, magic starts happening. Not mystical magic — practical magic. You start accessing parts of your creativity you didn't even know existed.

[B-ROLL: Transformative before/after footage showing enhanced creativity]

We've accepted friction as normal for too long. We've accepted that breaking our creative state is just part of the process. But what if it doesn't have to be?

[TALKING HEAD]

What if we had a complete creative ecosystem designed specifically for movement artists? What if flow state wasn't something you occasionally stumbled into, but something your tools actually helped you access?
