## CORE PROBLEM

**The Core Problem Priya Faces:**

Priya is a choreographer without a professional capture tool. She's building complex, intentional movement pieces — they require specific music, specific timing, specific energy — and she's trying to document them with tools that weren't designed for her craft. Every time she records, she's managing audio sync (her #1 time killer), noise restrictions (her apartment has thin walls), camera positioning (no reliable hands-free setup), and limited recording time (notifications interrupt takes, phone overheats on long sessions). The result: she can't reliably capture the truth of her choreography. What she sees in her head and what ends up on camera are different things — and not because of her movement, but because of her tools. She's a professional working with amateur infrastructure, and it's affecting her ability to iterate on her own work, build a teaching archive, and share what she creates with the quality her craft deserves.

4. **Anxiety about falling behind** — "Other choreographers are building bodies of work — documented, organized, reviewable. I have a camera roll of disconnected clips with bad audio that I can't use as a reference library. They're building an archive. I'm accumulating clutter."

5. **Resentment toward the technical layer** — "I didn't spend eight years developing my craft to spend my evenings troubleshooting audio sync in iMovie. Every hour I spend on technical problems is an hour I'm not choreographing, not teaching, not developing as an artist. The tools are stealing time from my art."

## OTHER SOLUTIONS

### What Priya Tried in the Past (5 Different Solutions)

1. **CapCut and iMovie for post-production audio sync** — Spent hours per video manually aligning audio to footage. Sync would drift halfway through longer pieces. The iterative review cycle (film → edit → review → adjust → re-film → re-edit) made a 2-minute choreography a weekend project.

2. **Bluetooth speaker in her apartment** — Neighbors complained twice. Building management sent a formal warning. She can now only use speakers during specific hours, which means creative impulses at 10PM or 7AM die on the vine.

3. **Renting studio space** — $40/hour. The time pressure made her stiff and anxious. She couldn't relax or iterate properly when the meter was running. Three sessions totaling $120 with mediocre results. Not sustainable.

4. **Asking Ethan to film** — He tried. He doesn't understand framing for dance. He'd zoom in during footwork. He'd accidentally stop recording. And it created tension between them because she'd get frustrated and he'd feel criticized. The audio sync issue still existed regardless of who held the camera.

5. **Batch filming multiple pieces in one session** — Filmed six choreographies in one Saturday. By piece four, her energy was drained, her expression was flat, and the last two pieces looked lifeless. She posted them anyway and they got half her normal engagement. The footage didn't capture the truth of the choreography because she was exhausted.

## MARKET SPECIFICS

### What Does the Market Hinge Their Success On?

Priya believes her development as a choreographer depends on:

1. **"I need reliable capture that matches the quality of my work."** — The footage needs to represent her choreography honestly. If the audio is off-sync, the video is a lie about her musicality. If the recording cuts short, the arc of the piece is broken. Reliable capture is the foundation of everything else.

2. **"I need a real review and iteration cycle."** — Film it, watch it, adjust it, film it again. This is how choreography gets refined. The cycle needs to be fast and frictionless, not a weekend project.

3. **"I need to build a professional body of work."** — Organized, searchable, presentable. Not a chaotic camera roll. A portfolio that represents her skill, her range, and her development over time.

4. **"I need to capture ideas when they come, not when conditions are perfect."** — Creative impulses don't wait for studio time. She needs to be able to run a piece at midnight in her apartment, at the park on a lunch break, at the studio between classes.

5. **"I need to be intentional about what music I work with."** — The right music doesn't just complement choreography — it elevates it. She wants to discover music with intention, find producers whose work resonates with her movement style, and build a real music library for her practice.

### What Does Priya Have to Give Up?

1. **Perfectionism as protection** — "I can't post because the quality isn't good enough" is a shield against judgment of her actual choreography. If the capture is professional, the only thing left to judge is the work itself. That's scarier.

2. **The comfort of potential** — "If I had the right tools, my work would be incredible." Removing the tools excuse means she either IS incredible or she has work to do. Both are more confronting than the fantasy.

3. **Moral superiority over "content dancers"** — There's a quiet satisfaction in being the "serious choreographer" who's too focused on craft to worry about documentation. Having professional tools means joining the documentation game — and admitting that documentation matters.

4. **The excuse to stay at her day job** — "I can't go full-time because my documentation isn't generating opportunities" is safe and logical. If her documentation becomes professional and opportunities start arriving, the only thing between her and full-time dance is her own courage.

5. **Ethan's sympathy** — Complaining about the technical struggle is a form of connection. He comforts her, validates her frustration. If the problem disappears, she loses that specific avenue of emotional support and has to find new ways to connect.
