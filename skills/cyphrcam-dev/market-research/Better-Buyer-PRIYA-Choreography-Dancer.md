# BETTER BUYER PROFILE: PRIYA — The Choreography Dancer

---

## DEMOGRAPHIC

**Name:** Priya  
**Age:** 29  
**Brief Description:** Full-time marketing associate at a mid-size agency. Teaches two hip-hop choreography classes per week at a local dance studio. Has 3,200 Instagram followers, mostly from her dance content. Lives with her boyfriend Ethan in a small apartment. Has been choreographing for eight years, started posting content seriously about three years ago. Makes about $400/month from teaching and $0 from content. Dreams of going full-time with dance but can't make the math work. Posts choreography videos every 10-14 days because the recording-to-editing process is so exhausting that she dreads it.

---

## CORE PROBLEM

**The Core Problem Priya Faces:**

Priya has the talent and the ideas — she choreographs routines in her head on her commute, hears a song and immediately starts mapping movement to it. But the actual process of turning those ideas into content is a nightmare. Between finding time to record (evening sessions after 9-to-5 work), managing audio sync (her #1 time killer), dealing with apartment noise restrictions, and editing footage into something that looks presentable — she spends roughly 4 hours of production work for every 1 minute of finished dance content. She knows she needs to post more frequently to grow her following and attract teaching opportunities, but the technical burden has turned content creation from something she loved into something she actively avoids. She watches other choreographers posting three times a week with clean, professional-looking content and can't figure out what they know that she doesn't.

---

## TOP 5 MOST POWERFUL EMOTIONS AROUND THAT PROBLEM

1. **Resentment** — "I spent FOUR HOURS editing a 45-second video last Sunday. Four hours I could have spent choreographing new pieces, teaching, spending time with Ethan, literally anything else. I'm starting to hate the thing I used to love because of the bullshit that surrounds it."

2. **Inadequacy** — "Other choreographers post three times a week and their content looks flawless. Am I just slower than everyone else? Do they have teams? Better equipment? Am I doing something fundamentally wrong that makes this so hard?"

3. **Guilt** — "Ethan hasn't said anything, but I can tell he's over it. Every weekend I'm either filming or editing while he sits on the couch watching TV alone. I chose dance over our Sunday breakfast AGAIN last week. I'm being a shitty partner."

4. **Anxiety** — "I'm 29. I have maybe 5-6 good years to make this work before the algorithm stops caring about my age bracket. Every week I don't post is a week I'm falling behind. The window is closing and I'm stuck editing audio."

5. **Exhaustion** — "I'm tired. Work is draining, commuting is draining, teaching is draining in a different way, and then I'm supposed to come home and turn into a one-person production studio? Some nights I just stare at my phone and think 'I can't do this anymore.'"

## TOP 5 BIGGEST FEARS

1. **"I'm going to age out of relevance before I ever get a real shot."** — She watches 21-year-old choreographers go viral and knows the industry skews young. Every birthday feels like a countdown timer. She imagines being 35, still teaching two classes a week at the same studio, still working the same marketing job, and realizing the window closed while she was busy syncing audio in iMovie.

2. **"My content will always look amateur compared to the people I'm competing with."** — She follows choreographers with cinematic-quality videos — smooth audio, multiple angles, color grading. Her content looks like it was shot in someone's apartment because it WAS shot in someone's apartment. She's terrified that no matter how good her choreography is, the packaging will always hold her back.

3. **"I'm going to lose Ethan because I can't balance this."** — Ethan has been patient. Really patient. But she can hear the edge in his voice when she says "I just need one more hour to finish this edit." She's seen relationships fall apart over less. She's terrified that she'll sacrifice her relationship for a dance career that never materializes.

4. **"I'll never make enough from dance to justify leaving my day job."** — $400/month from teaching. Zero from content. She'd need at least $4,000/month to replace her salary. That feels impossible with her current output rate. She's terrified that dance will always be a side hustle that takes main-character energy from her life.

5. **"People will figure out that my choreography isn't that original."** — She worries that her pieces are derivative. That someone will comment "this looks like [other choreographer]'s style" and everyone will agree. The fear of being exposed as unoriginal keeps her from posting work she's actually proud of, which means she only posts "safe" pieces that don't push boundaries.

## OTHER SOLUTIONS

### What Priya Tried in the Past (5 Different Solutions)

1. **CapCut and iMovie for post-production audio sync** — Hours spent manually aligning audio to footage. Sometimes she'd get it 90% right and then notice the sync drifts halfway through. Start over. She's lost entire weekends to this process.

2. **Bluetooth speaker in her apartment while filming** — Neighbors knocked on her door twice. Building management sent a formal warning. She can only practice at certain hours now and even then she keeps the volume low, which means the recorded audio is barely audible and mixed with room noise.

3. **Renting studio space for filming** — $40/hour at the cheapest spot in her city. She tried it three times. The time pressure of paying by the hour made her anxious and stiff on camera. She couldn't relax knowing the meter was running. Plus coordinating schedules was a nightmare.

4. **Batch filming multiple pieces in one session** — Filmed six choreographies in one Saturday afternoon. By piece four, her energy was gone, her makeup was melting, and the last two videos looked dead. She posted them anyway and they got half the engagement of her normal content. Never again.

5. **Asking Ethan to film her** — He tried. He really did. But he doesn't understand framing for dance. He'd zoom in on her face during footwork sections. He'd accidentally stop recording mid-routine. And the audio sync issue still existed. Plus it created tension between them because she'd get frustrated and he'd feel criticized.

## MARKET SPECIFICS

### What Does the Market Hinge Their Success On?

Priya believes her success as a choreographer depends on:

1. **"I need to post more consistently."** — She's convinced that the algorithm rewards volume and consistency above all else. If she could just post 3x/week instead of 2x/month, everything would change. The bottleneck is production time, not creative ideas.

2. **"My content needs to look and sound professional."** — She believes the quality gap between her content and top choreographers' content is the main reason she's not growing. If her audio was clean and her footage looked polished, people would see how good her choreography actually is.

3. **"I need more teaching hours to build my reputation."** — More classes = more students = more word of mouth = more followers = more opportunities. But she can't get more classes until she proves she can bring people in through content. It's a chicken-and-egg problem.

4. **"I need to remove the production bottleneck."** — She's correctly identified that the recording-to-posting pipeline is what's holding her back. If that pipeline was frictionless, everything downstream would improve.

5. **"I need to prove to myself (and everyone else) that this can be a real career."** — Not just a hobby. Not just a side thing. An actual career with actual income. Until the numbers prove it, everyone — including herself — will treat it as optional.

### What Does Priya Have to Give Up?

The hidden satisfactions she gets from her current problem:

1. **Perfectionism as protection** — "I can't post because the quality isn't good enough" is a socially acceptable reason to avoid judgment. If she could post professional-quality content easily, she'd lose her excuse for not posting. And then if it doesn't perform well, the problem is her choreography — not her equipment.

2. **The "I could if I wanted to" fantasy** — Right now she can believe: "If I had the right tools and enough time, I'd be huge." Removing those barriers means she either IS huge or she isn't. The fantasy might be more comfortable than the reality.

3. **Moral superiority over "content creators"** — There's a subtle satisfaction in being the "serious artist" who's above the content treadmill. She looks down on choreographers who prioritize content over craft. Joining the content game means admitting that the game matters.

4. **Ethan's sympathy** — When she complains about the production struggle, Ethan is supportive and understanding. That sympathy is a form of connection between them. If the problem disappears, she loses that specific avenue of being comforted and validated by her partner.

5. **A reason to stay at her safe day job** — "I can't afford to go full-time because my content isn't generating income" is a logical, inarguable excuse. If her content suddenly blows up and starts making money, the only thing standing between her and quitting her job is courage. And that's terrifying.

### Top 5 Biggest Objections Priya Might Have

1. **"Will this actually produce content that looks professional enough for my brand?"** — She's not posting anything that looks amateur. If the output quality isn't at least as good as what she's painstakingly creating now, it's a downgrade. She needs to see that the footage meets her standards.

2. **"I don't have time to learn another tool."** — She's already drowning. If this has a steep learning curve, it becomes one more thing on her plate. She needs it to be dead simple from the first time she opens it.

3. **"I'm skeptical of apps that claim to solve everything."** — She's tried "game-changing" tools before. CapCut promised easy editing. It wasn't easy. InShot promised quick results. The results were mediocre. She's been burned by marketing promises.

4. **"What if it works for freestylers but not for choreography?"** — A lot of dance tools are clearly designed for improv/freestyle. Her needs are different — she needs to run specific choreography to specific songs, often multiple takes, and the audio needs to be perfect every time.

5. **"My boyfriend is going to think I'm buying another thing I won't use."** — She's got two ring lights, a tripod she used three times, and a lavalier mic that's still in the packaging. Adding another purchase to the "dance tool graveyard" in their closet would prove everyone right about this being a money pit.
