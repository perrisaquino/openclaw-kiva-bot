# BETTER BUYER PROFILE: NIKO — The Freestyle Dancer

---

## DEMOGRAPHIC

**Name:** Niko  
**Age:** 26  
**Brief Description:** Works a 9-to-5 at a tech startup doing customer support. Lives in a one-bedroom apartment with thin walls. Started freestyling about two years ago after watching videos online, practices alone in his living room at night with headphones on. Has maybe 800 Instagram followers, posts dance clips occasionally but deletes most of them within 24 hours. Knows he's got something but can't shake the feeling that he's fooling himself. Single. Spends his lunch breaks watching freestylers on YouTube and wondering if he'll ever be that good.

---

## CORE PROBLEM

**The Core Problem Niko Faces:**

Niko is a dancer trapped inside a non-dancer's life. He feels the music deeply — he KNOWS he has something to express — but every time he tries to capture it on video, the result doesn't match what he felt in his body. The technical friction of recording (propping up his phone, the audio never syncing, the lighting being garbage, watching it back and cringing) has slowly poisoned the one thing that makes him feel alive. He's starting to associate dancing with disappointment instead of joy. And underneath all of it, he's terrified that the gap between how he FEELS when he dances and how he LOOKS when he watches it back means he's been deluding himself this whole time.

---

## TOP 5 MOST POWERFUL EMOTIONS AROUND THAT PROBLEM

1. **Self-doubt** — "Every time I watch my footage back, I think... is this actually good? Or do I just THINK it's good because I felt something? Maybe I'm the dance equivalent of a bad American Idol audition — the person who genuinely thinks they're talented but everyone's cringing."

2. **Frustration** — "I just had the most incredible round. I felt every single beat. I was locked in. Then I check my phone and the audio is off by two seconds, or the phone fell, or I'm half out of frame. That moment is gone forever and I can't get it back."

3. **Loneliness** — "I don't know any other freestylers in real life. All my friends think I'm weird for being this into it. I practice alone, I watch videos alone, I have no one to share this with who actually gets it."

4. **Shame** — "I'm 26 years old practicing dance moves in my apartment by myself. My parents think I should be focused on my career. My ex thought it was 'cute' at first and then started rolling her eyes. Sometimes I feel like a grown man playing pretend."

5. **Desperation** — "I can feel myself losing motivation. Every bad recording chips away at it. If I can't figure out how to actually capture what I'm doing and see real progress, I'm going to stop. And if I stop dancing, I don't know what I have left that's mine."

## TOP 5 BIGGEST FEARS

1. **"I'm actually not talented and everyone can see it except me."** — The American Idol fear. The nightmare scenario where he finally shares a video with someone he respects in the dance community and they watch it with that polite, tight-lipped smile that means "this is bad but I don't want to hurt your feelings." He replays that imaginary scenario at 2 AM more than he'd ever admit.

2. **"I'm going to wake up at 35 and realize I wasted my twenties on a hobby that went nowhere."** — He pictures himself at a work happy hour, someone asks what he does for fun, and he says "I used to dance" with this dead look in his eyes. Used to. Past tense. The dream quietly buried under years of excuses and bad recordings.

3. **"People at work will find my dance videos and I'll become a joke."** — His coworker Tyler already makes jokes about TikTok dancers. If Tyler found Niko's Instagram... the thought makes his stomach drop. He'd never recover from being "the guy who thinks he's a dancer" at the office.

4. **"I'll never find my own style and I'll always just be copying other people."** — He watches dancers like the ones in BTM who have this unmistakable identity in how they move, and he can't figure out how they got there. He's terrified that he's permanently stuck at "imitation" and will never reach "authentic."

5. **"The one thing that makes me feel like myself will become another thing I quit."** — Dance is the only time Niko feels fully present and alive. If the frustration of recording and the self-doubt about his skills kill that feeling, he doesn't have a backup. There's no Plan B for feeling connected to himself.

## OTHER SOLUTIONS

### What Niko Tried in the Past (5 Different Solutions)

1. **Propping his phone against a wall and playing music through a Bluetooth speaker** — Audio was tinny, echo from the room made it sound like he was dancing in a bathroom, and half the time his phone slid and he'd lose the shot.

2. **Screen recording with music from Spotify while filming with another device** — Tried to sync two separate recordings in iMovie. Took 45 minutes per video. Audio was always slightly off. Gave up after three attempts.

3. **Recording without music and adding it in post** — Danced in silence, tried to overlay the track afterward. The timing never felt right because he wasn't actually hearing the music when he danced. The videos looked mechanical and disconnected.

4. **Going to an open gym/studio session** — Went twice. Both times he stood in the corner too intimidated to actually dance because everyone else seemed way better. Paid $15 each time to basically watch other people practice.

5. **Posting raw phone videos with bad audio and hoping people would "get it"** — Posted a few clips with the speaker audio barely audible and background noise drowning everything out. Got 12 likes — mostly from family. Deleted them within 48 hours.

### Brief Conversational Soundbites About Failed Past Solutions

- **Phone against wall:** "I'm literally risking my $1200 phone every time I lean it against a shoe. And then the angle makes me look like I'm four feet tall."

- **Two-device sync:** "I spent more time in iMovie than I did dancing. And after all that, the sync was still off by like half a beat. Half a beat sounds small but when you're hitting a pocket it ruins everything."

- **Dancing in silence:** "You can tell I wasn't hearing the music. My body was guessing. It looked like I was doing choreography to the wrong song. Because I basically was."

- **Open gym:** "Everyone was in their zone and I was in the corner holding my water bottle like a tourist. I paid thirty dollars total to feel like an imposter twice."

- **Raw phone videos:** "I posted it, checked my notifications obsessively for an hour, got 12 likes, and deleted it at 1 AM feeling stupid. My aunt commented 'so talented!!' which somehow made it worse."

Not just feel it — see it. He has a Tuesday from three months ago and a Tuesday from last week and the difference is undeniable. That visual proof silences the self-doubt.

He doesn't delete it after 24 hours. He leaves it up. People he doesn't know start following him.

5. **"I found my own style because I could finally focus on MOVING instead of managing technology."** — Without the friction of setup and editing, his practice sessions became about the dance itself. He stopped overthinking and started trusting his body. His movements became his — not copies of other people. He can watch his footage and see a style emerging that's unmistakably Niko.

## MARKET SPECIFICS

### What Does the Market Hinge Their Success On?

Niko believes his success as a freestyle dancer depends on:

1. **"I need to be able to see myself clearly."** — He's convinced that if he could just get reliable, good-quality footage of his practice, he'd improve twice as fast. The recording problem isn't just annoying — it's the bottleneck holding back his entire development.

2. **"I need the audio to match what I heard when I danced."** — The gap between what he hears in his headphones and what shows up on video is the single most frustrating thing. If the music on the recording matched his experience, everything would change.

3. **"I need to practice more consistently without it being a production every time."** — He believes volume and repetition will unlock his style. But the setup friction means he practices less than he wants to, so he never builds momentum.

4. **"I need to find other freestylers who are at my level."** — He thinks community and feedback would accelerate his growth. But he can't share footage because the quality is embarrassing, which keeps him isolated.

5. **"I need to not overthink it."** — He knows his self-consciousness is the biggest barrier, but he can't just "stop thinking about it." He needs the external friction to disappear so the internal friction has less fuel.

### What Does Niko Have to Give Up?

The hidden satisfactions he gets from his current problem:

1. **The safety of potential** — As long as he never really puts himself out there with quality footage, he can keep believing he's secretly amazing. Bad recordings give him an excuse: "If the video actually captured what I felt, people would see how good I am." Solving the recording problem means he has to confront whether the dancing itself is actually as good as he feels it is.

2. **The identity of the misunderstood artist** — Being the guy whose talent is unrecognized gives him a certain romantic narrative. He's the hidden gem. The undiscovered talent. If he's discovered and it turns out he's just... okay... he loses that narrative.

3. **The excuse for not posting** — "My setup sucks" is a convenient reason to avoid the vulnerability of sharing his work publicly. Without that excuse, the only thing between him and posting is courage. And that's scarier.

4. **Comfortable isolation** — Practicing alone means no criticism, no comparison, no judgment. It's safe. Solving his problem and becoming visible means opening himself to all of those things.

5. **Anger as fuel** — Being frustrated with technology gives him something external to blame. That frustration is actually energizing in a weird way. Without it, he'd have to sit with the quieter, scarier question: "Am I doing enough?"

2. **"What if I start using this and I realize I'm actually not that good?"** — This is the deepest objection and the one he'd never say out loud. Better recordings mean clearer footage. Clearer footage means harder-to-deny reality about his current skill level.

4. **"What if it's complicated to set up and ruins my flow?"** — His whole problem is that technology gets between him and dancing. If this app adds MORE friction instead of removing it, it defeats the entire purpose.

5. **"Is this really for freestylers like me, or is it designed for choreo people?"** — Most dance tools feel like they were made for choreographers. If this is another "learn this routine" app disguised as a recording tool, he's not interested.
