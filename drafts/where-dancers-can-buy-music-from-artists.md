# Where Dancers Can Buy Music Directly From Artists

I've been thinking about this since before CyphrCam even had a name.

When I was figuring out how the app should work, one of the first questions I had to sit with was: where does the music come from? CyphrCam syncs your music to your footage automatically, but you bring your own tracks. That's by design. I'm not trying to build another streaming platform. I'm building a tool for movement.

But that raised a real question. Where are dancers actually getting their music? And more specifically, where should they be getting it if they want to own the files and actually use them?

I'll be honest about what got me here. I was at the Japanese Garden in Portland once, just walking around, and I had this impulse to dance. I had my headphones on, the music was right, the space was beautiful. I recorded it. And then when I watched it back, the footage was silent. The music that was there for the movement wasn't there for the video. That disconnect bothered me more than I expected it to.

Building CyphrCam was my answer to that problem. But as soon as the app could actually work, another question showed up: the music people dance to, where does it come from, and who's getting paid for it?

I'm not going to pretend I don't know how people move music around online. But I've also been sitting with something: the artists whose music we dance to are often independent, often underpaid, and often making something genuinely singular that moves us in ways we struggle to explain. They deserve support. Most of us know that, even if we don't always act on it.

So here's a practical post. Here's where you can actually buy music, own the files, and bring them into CyphrCam or anywhere else you need them.

---

## First, The Problem with Streaming

Spotify, Apple Music, Tidal, all of them: streaming is great for discovering music. It's how I find most of what I dance to. But you don't own any of it. You can't download a Spotify track as an audio file and import it anywhere. You're renting, not buying.

CyphrCam needs actual audio files to work. MP3, WAV, M4A. When you dance with headphones and want the music to auto-sync to your footage, you need a file you own and can access from your phone's music library or import directly into the app.

So the gap is real: you heard a track on Spotify, you want to dance to it, but you can't get the file. What do you do?

Here's what I've been doing, and what I'd recommend.

---

## Bandcamp

If I could only point dancers to one place, it would be Bandcamp.

Bandcamp is a platform built specifically for independent artists to sell their music directly to fans. When you buy a track or album there, the artist keeps a significant cut, far more than any streaming royalty. And you get the actual file, in your choice of format: MP3, FLAC, WAV, AAC, and more.

For dancers, this is especially useful. A lot of the music I've noticed people freestyling to, particularly in the underground, comes from independent producers and artists who are on Bandcamp. If you've ever heard a track at a cypher and thought "where is this from," there's a real chance it lives on Bandcamp somewhere.

**What you're getting:** Downloadable files in multiple formats. The higher quality options (FLAC, WAV) are genuinely useful if you care about audio fidelity during recording.

**Price range:** Albums usually run $5 to $15. Individual tracks can be $1 to $3. Some artists let you pay what you want, including free.

**Getting it into CyphrCam:** Download the file to your computer, sync it to your phone via the Finder, or AirDrop it directly. From there it shows up in your music library and CyphrCam can access it. There's also a Bandcamp app where you can download purchases straight to your phone, which is the easiest path if you're already set up there.

One thing worth knowing: Bandcamp runs "Bandcamp Fridays" periodically where they waive their revenue share and 100% goes directly to the artist. If you're going to buy anyway, timing it to one of those days makes a difference.

## iTunes / Apple Music Purchases

This one gets overlooked because people mix up Apple Music (the streaming subscription) with iTunes purchases (actually buying a track).

Here's the distinction: even if you have an Apple Music subscription, you can still go into the iTunes Store and buy individual tracks or albums outright. You own them. They live in your library permanently. They don't disappear if you cancel your subscription.

For dancers on iPhone, which I'm guessing is most of you if you're using CyphrCam, this is probably the most frictionless option. You buy the track, it goes straight into your Apple Music library, and CyphrCam can pull from that directly.

**What you're getting:** DRM-protected AAC files at 256kbps. Not lossless, but solid quality for most recording uses.

**Price range:** $1.29 per track typically, $9.99 to $12.99 for most albums.

**Getting it into CyphrCam:** It's already in your Apple Music library. CyphrCam can access it from there. No extra steps needed.

The limitation is catalog. If you're looking for underground or independent artists, Bandcamp will almost always have more. But for mainstream tracks or anything with wide distribution, iTunes purchases are quick and clean.

## Qobuz

I came across Qobuz while researching high-quality audio options for CyphrCam users who care about that side of things. Qobuz is a streaming and download platform that specializes in lossless and hi-res audio.

If you're recording video and you're particular about audio quality, Qobuz is worth knowing about. You can buy individual tracks or albums as FLAC downloads, which is about as clean as it gets for digital audio.

**What you're getting:** Lossless FLAC files, sometimes at higher resolution than CD quality (24-bit / 96kHz or higher).

**Price range:** Individual tracks around $1.29 to $2.99. Albums $8 to $20 depending on resolution and catalog. They also have a subscription that includes discounts on purchases.

**Getting it into CyphrCam:** Download the FLAC to your computer, convert to MP3 or AAC if needed (CyphrCam works with standard formats), then sync to your phone via Finder. A few more steps than iTunes, but worth it if audio fidelity matters to you.

Qobuz leans toward jazz, classical, and well-produced pop and soul. If that's your movement world, this one's worth bookmarking.

---

## Amazon Music / Amazon Digital Purchases

Amazon has a digital music store that often gets skipped over. Through Amazon Music, you can buy individual tracks and albums as MP3 downloads that you own outright.

The catalog is broad and comparable to iTunes. Prices are often similar or slightly lower. If you have Amazon Prime, you occasionally get deals on digital purchases.

**What you're getting:** MP3 files at 256kbps. Comparable to iTunes in terms of quality.

**Price range:** Around $1.29 per track, $5 to $13 for albums. Occasional sales.

**Getting it into CyphrCam:** Download to your computer, transfer to phone via Finder or AirDrop, then import into CyphrCam. Or use the Amazon Music app to access purchases directly on your phone.

This isn't my first recommendation, but it's solid for mainstream catalog and sometimes has pricing advantages over iTunes.

## Artist Websites and Patreon

This is the most direct route, and the one that supports artists the most.

A lot of producers and musicians sell music directly from their own websites, often through Gumroad, their own Shopify store, or a simple link in their bio. You pay them directly, you get the file. No platform taking a cut beyond payment processing.

Patreon works differently but lands in a similar place. Some artists offer downloadable tracks, stems, or instrumentals to their Patreon supporters at certain tiers. If there's a producer or artist whose music you keep coming back to, checking their Patreon is worth a look.

I've noticed this more with producers in the hip-hop and dance music space. They'll drop instrumental packs, exclusive tracks, and early releases through direct channels because they've built enough of a following to go around the platforms.

**What you're getting:** Varies. Could be MP3, WAV, or stems. Usually the artist specifies.

**Price range:** Varies widely. Direct sales might be $1 to $10 per track. Patreon tiers might be $5 to $20 per month for access to exclusive content.

**Getting it into CyphrCam:** Download the file, transfer to your phone, import or sync. Standard process.

If you're building a movement practice around specific artists' music, this is the relationship worth investing in. You're funding the work directly.

## SoundCloud

SoundCloud is more of a discovery platform than a store, but some artists enable free or paid downloads on their tracks.

I use SoundCloud to find music I wouldn't find anywhere else. Underground producers, work-in-progress tracks, genre-blending stuff that's too niche for mainstream platforms. Some of it has a download button enabled by the artist, meaning they want you to have it.

**What you're getting:** MP3 typically, at varying bitrates. Not always the highest quality, but often the only place to get certain tracks.

**Price range:** Often free when downloads are enabled. Some tracks are "name your price."

**Getting it into CyphrCam:** If the download is available, grab it, transfer to your phone, import. SoundCloud downloads usually go to your device's download folder.

One thing to keep in mind: not every track on SoundCloud with a download button has been cleared by whoever owns the rights. If it's the artist's own page and they're offering the download, you're good. If it's a random repost or fan upload, it's murkier. Pay attention to whose page you're on.

## The Bigger Picture

Here's what I keep coming back to when I think about all this.

The music we dance to is somebody's creative output. It took time, money, equipment, and usually years of developing an ear and a sense of sound. When we buy that music directly from artists, we're participating in a cycle that makes more of it possible.

I built CyphrCam because I wanted to capture movement honestly. When you're in the zone with headphones on, that moment is real. The music is part of it. It's not separate from the movement; it's what the movement is responding to.

So it felt right to be clear about where that music should come from. Not because I think everyone has to buy every track, but because I want the culture around CyphrCam to be one that actually values what it uses.

If you care about dancers being taken seriously as artists, extending that same care to musicians makes sense. The two worlds are more connected than they sometimes feel.

---

## Where to Start

If this is new to you, here's the short version:

Start with Bandcamp. Make an account, search for artists you already love, and see what's there. A lot of independent music you didn't know was purchasable is available there, often for less than you'd expect.

For mainstream tracks, iTunes purchases are the cleanest path if you're on iPhone since the files go straight into your library and CyphrCam can access them without any extra steps.

If an artist you love has a website or Patreon, check it. The direct purchase is almost always the best option for them and for you.

That's it. No grand statement about how people get their music, no lecture. Just: here's where you can actually buy music and own the files, if you want to do that.

If you have a platform or resource I missed, I'd genuinely like to know about it.
